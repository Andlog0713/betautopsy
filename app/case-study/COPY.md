## 3. What I built

**BetAutopsy: a forensic report on how you actually bet.**

Not a tracker. Not a picks service. A diagnostic. You upload a CSV of your bet history from any major book or DFS operator, and within a couple of minutes you get back a personalized breakdown — written in the voice of a slightly grim coroner.

**Upload — CSV in, report out.** DraftKings, FanDuel, PrizePicks, Underdog, any sportsbook export. No manual logging. No friction at the top of the funnel.

**Behavioral analysis — reads patterns, not just outcomes.** ROI is table stakes. The product surfaces tilt windows, stake creep after losses, parlay overuse, sport-specific leaks, and odds drift — the stuff bettors feel but can't name.

**Scores — Tilt Index, Discipline Score, ROI.** Three numbers a bettor can argue with their friends about. Designed to be screenshot-worthy and re-runnable next month.

**Bet DNA — archetypes that travel.** Each user gets pegged to a behavioral archetype — Tilt Merchant, Public Square, Sharp-In-Training. Built for organic share, not just analysis.

**Pricing — three tiers, priced for the curve I'm actually selling to.**
- Free / $0 — snapshot, top-of-funnel hook
- Pro / $19 — full report, the workhorse SKU
- Sharp / $39 — power user, recurring lifecycle

Free exists to fill the top of the funnel and prove the insight. Pro is the workhorse — priced where a bettor will expense it against one bad weekend. Sharp is the recurring tier for the bettor who's actually trying to fix something, and the one I'm optimizing lifecycle around.

## 4. Distribution

**I went to market like an operator, not a founder.**

The thesis: PrizePicks-style creative outperforms sportsbook creative on Meta because it sells outcome (a bigger payout, a cleaner story) instead of feature (boost, parlay, same-game). I've watched it work for two and a half years. So I built the BetAutopsy ad library around that insight, not around "upload your bets."

**Meta — paid social.** Primary acquisition channel. Creative built in the PrizePicks ad-library voice (high-contrast, single-frame, outcome-led) rather than the sportsbook house style (offer-led, logo-heavy). Hypothesis: same audience, different conversion intent — the diagnostic frame outperforms the deposit frame.

**TikTok — organic + paid.** Organic-first to learn what hooks land before spending. Posting reaction-style breakdowns of bettors' own data with the forensic voice. Paid spend follows whichever organic angles get pickup, not the other way around.

**Reddit — r/prizepicks GTM.** I worked at PrizePicks. That earns one credibility-led launch post in r/prizepicks, not a marketing blast — the moderator team and the community both know how to smell a paid campaign. The angle is "former PP employee shipped a tool to read your own play." Honest, on-brand, and the only place where my employer history is a distribution asset rather than a liability.

**Cold email — 5K bettors.** A targeted 5,000-bettor outbound sequence, sourced from public sports-betting communities and DFS leaderboards. Three-touch sequence, opt-out respected on first request, single CTA: run your autopsy free. CRM hygiene > volume.

## 5. The PrizePicks chapter

**2.5 years running paid social, lifecycle, and CRM at a regulated DFS operator.**

Primary KPIs were CPA, CAC, and ROAS, against **Placed a Lineup** as the primary conversion event. I ran Meta as the primary channel, plus TikTok, Snapchat, and Reddit. Creative strategy and audience strategy on the paid side. Lifecycle and reactivation on the CRM side, in Braze.

### Signature win — lifecycle reactivation

**The reactivation creative wasn't built for the user it was being served to.**

*Diagnosis.* The Braze reactivation push was reusing new-user-offer creative — first-deposit framing, "welcome to PrizePicks," the kind of copy a lapsed existing user reads as either condescending or irrelevant. Open rates and reactivation lift were soft and trending in the wrong direction.

*Rebuild.* I rewrote the creative for the existing-user context: acknowledge that the user already knows the product, reference the specific sports calendar they last played, no acquisition-style offer. The voice shifted from "come try this" to "you're missing the slate you usually play."

*Test.* A/B against the incumbent creative on a shorter lapsed window than the previous evergreen — the hypothesis being that recently-lapsed users had higher reactivation intent and shorter recall, so the existing-user framing would compound. The new creative won on [TK — primary metric: reactivation rate / open / placed-a-lineup lift].

*Outcome.* It went evergreen and is **still running today, after I left the company**. The strongest signal I could ask for that the diagnosis was right — the org kept the work in market without me there to defend it.

**Why it matters for a CRM / lifecycle role:** it wasn't a copywriting fix. The actual diagnosis was that the segmentation model was wrong — the team was serving lapsed users an acquisition message because the audience was defined by recency, not by user state. The creative was the symptom. That's the kind of pattern I look for first.
